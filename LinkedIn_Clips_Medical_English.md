## CLIP 2 — "What Does 'Well Nourished' Actually Mean?"

**Timestamps:** 6:43 – 7:55 (~70 seconds)

**Transcript:**

> **Speaker 2:** So "well nourished" — do you guys use the word "habitus" in Portuguese to explain like, the shape of a patient or how they look? [...] So a well nourished patient is one that is not failing to thrive, which means if you don't have failure to thrive, you're not nutritionally malnourished. It's by appearance. So you're talking about habitus, but you're talking about someone who doesn't have cachexia because they have cancer, they don't have hypothyroid, hyperthyroid, they are not eating enough. And it's a very specific habitus and a series of symptoms, but it's a general feeling, like when you say that a patient is well appearing. What you're not saying is, I know that they're perfectly healthy, but what you're saying is that they LOOK healthy. So this is like saying, you look nourished, you look like you have enough food, you look like you've eaten, you know, drunk enough water.

**LinkedIn Caption:**

📋 "Well nourished" — what does it really mean in a patient chart?

When you read "well nourished" in a physical exam, it doesn't mean the patient is perfectly healthy.

It means: they LOOK like they're getting adequate nutrition.

➡️ No signs of cachexia
➡️ No failure to thrive
➡️ General appearance suggests adequate food and hydration

It's about HABITUS — the patient's general appearance — not a nutritional diagnosis.

This is one of those terms that trips up medical English learners because it sounds vague, but it has a specific clinical meaning.

The trick? Think of the Z sound in "zebra."

No hard G. No S sound. Just a lazy, soft "zh" in the middle.

You aren't born knowing this — and asking is the smartest thing you can do.

## CLIP 4 — "'Atraumatic' — What It Really Means"

**Timestamps:** 8:51 – 10:30 (~100 seconds)

**Transcript:**

> **Speaker 2:** So the head is normal, cephalic, which is to say that it's basically the normal size and shape. Cephalic being head, normal meaning normal. So "atraumatic" meaning, there are no lesions, and there's no damage or bruising. So basically it's appearing not just that it's well, but that it has not been physically injured. Like if somebody got stabbed in the head or something, that would be a traumatic event. Trauma, referring to damage, as opposed to an illness process. So "atraumatic" — no trauma. It appears that it has no trauma.
>
> And then no visible or palpable masses. A palpable mass is just something that you can feel with your finger — whether it's cystic, whether it's fluctuant like an abscess, it's still a mass. Palpable mass. And then depressions are actually indentations. A depression — a lot of babies will have scalping where the formation of their head is a little different because of how it came out of the vaginal canal. That would be a depression of their skull at that location.

**LinkedIn Caption:**

🧠 "Normocephalic, atraumatic" — let's break this down.

You'll see this on almost every physical exam, but what does it actually mean?

NORMOCEPHALIC = the head is normal size and shape
ATRAUMATIC = no signs of physical injury (no lesions, no bruising)

"A-" = without
"Traumatic" = related to trauma/physical damage

So "atraumatic" means: this head has NOT been physically injured.

It does NOT mean there's no disease — it means there's no trauma.

That distinction matters when you're learning Medical English.

## CLIP 5 — "Trauma vs. Depression — Two Very Different Words"

**Timestamps:** 10:30 – 12:39 (~130 seconds)

**Transcript:**

> **Speaker 3:** What is the difference between "atraumatic" and "depression"? Because I think depression is related to something genetic or...
>
> **Speaker 2:** They're very different concepts. So if you had like a gunshot, if somebody shot you, that would be trauma, because it would cause damage to the tissue. You're talking about something that happens to you — if you fell off of a tree and hit your head, that would be trauma to your head from the tree. So this is definitely not congenital. This is trauma. So if it looks like there's blood, if there's a cut, we can call that a lesion as well in English.
>
> And then a depression — technically speaking, a depression is just an indentation. That just means: this is the surface that you would expect, and it is lower. That's all. Not an ulceration. An ulceration is not a depression. An ulcer is slightly different. A depression is just very technically... it's going like this — a little bit lower.
>
> So that's a very good question, because these are very technical words.

**LinkedIn Caption:**

❓ "Trauma" vs. "Depression" in a clinical context — do you know the difference?

These words mean something VERY different in a physical exam:

TRAUMA = physical damage from an external force (gunshot, fall, stabbing)
DEPRESSION = an indentation — a surface that is lower than expected

⚠️ A depression is NOT an ulceration.
⚠️ "Depression" here has NOTHING to do with mental health.

In Medical English, context changes everything. These are very technical words, and asking questions is how you learn them.

## CLIP 6 — "Latin Plurals: Fundus → Fundi"

**Timestamps:** 12:55 – 14:20 (~85 seconds)

**Transcript:**

> **Speaker 2:** A lot of people have trouble with this, but for the most part — how would you say more than one octopus? You would say octopi, right. Don't ask me why. I think it's because it's like Greek or something. So octopus — eight-legged — and if there's more than one, it's going to be octopi. You change the P-U-S into P-I, right?
>
> And so what is the original singular form? It's fundus. So it's just like octopus — fundi. So more than one, plural form. And you can have a fundus of the gallbladder, you can have a fundus of the stomach, and you can have a fundus of the eye. The fundus is where you'll find the retina.
>
> I would bet American medical students don't necessarily know that word, but it's a good word.

**LinkedIn Caption:**

📖 Fundus → Fundi. Do you know your Latin plurals?

Medical English is full of Latin and Greek roots, and the plurals don't follow normal English rules.

The trick? Think of "octopus → octopi."

Same pattern:
🔹 Fundus → Fundi
🔹 Bronchus → Bronchi
🔹 Nucleus → Nuclei

Fun fact: You can have a fundus of the gallbladder, the stomach, AND the eye.

Even American med students don't always know this — so don't feel bad for asking!

## CLIP 7 — "On Time is Late" — US Hospital Culture

**Transcript:**

>

**LinkedIn Caption:**

⏰ "In America, on time is late."

This is the #1 culture shock for international medical graduates doing rotations in the U.S.

Here's the hierarchy:
🟢 Early = on time
🟡 On time = late
🔴 Late = you're in trouble

And if you ARE late? Don't explain. Just say:
"I'm sorry. It won't happen again."

That's it. No excuses.

The REAL advice for nailing your presentations? Show up early. Find someone who has already presented to YOUR attending. Ask for the format that attending likes.

Every physician wants something different — and the only way to know is to ASK.
